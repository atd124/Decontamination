Fentanyl is the 'Single Greatest Challenge' Facing the US

Fentanyl, a man-made opiod 50 times more potent than herion, is now the leading cause of death of American adults under 50.

By First Line Technology

April 25, 2023

Fentanyl and other opioids are causing the worst drug crisis in American history. During a recent Senate hearing, Department of Homeland Security Secretary Alejandro Mayorkas described fentanyl as the "single greatest challenge” facing the US.

The numbers are staggering

According to the latest CDC data, a record 80,411 Americans overdosed on opioids in 2021. Most of them, 70,601, died from fentanyl or similar man-made opioids. When President Nixon launched the first war on drugs in 1971 and declared drug abuse “public enemy number one” 6,771 Americans overdosed.





The numbers are staggering, and not just in contrast to 1971. On average, 195 people overdose on fentanyl every day; the equivalent of a Boeing 757 crashing and killing everyone on board. Daily. In 2021, fentanyl overdoses exceeded deaths from HIV (1995), car crashes (1972) and gun violence (1993)—at their peaks. It is now the leading cause of death of American adults under 50.

But fentanyl isn't new. First developed in 1959 and introduced in the 1960s as an intravenous pain killer, it was available when Nixon declared war on drugs half a century ago; so why are so many Americans dying now?

How did this happen?





THE FIRST WAVE

The opiod epidemic started in 1999, when Doctors began over prescribing legal opioids like OxyContin. At the time, New Mexico had the highest per capita overdose rate (10.2).





In 2020, OxyContin manufacturer Purdue Pharma agreed to plead guilty to three felony counts and reached an $8.3 billion settlement with Department of Justice for its role in the opioid crisis. (Yuri Gripas/Pool via AP)



THE SECOND WAVE

By 2010, as the epidimic spreads beyond the Southwest border, addicts, especially in Appalachia, begin to turn to heroin, a stronger alternative.

THE THIRD WAVE

Then, in 2013, the third wave begins, with significant increases in overdose deaths involving synthetic opioids, primarily fentanyl.

TODAY

For a sense of the magnitude of the problem and how quickly it changed, consider West Virginia. In 1999, when the crisis started, the per capita overdose death rate was 1.8. Now it's 77.2.

















“One of the most striking things we found in the course of reporting this is that heroin has almost disappeared from illegal drug markets in the United States, and it's been almost entirely replaced by fentanyl.”





Exponentially more lethal and potent

Fentanyl kills more people than any other opioid, including prescription pills and heroin, because it is so easy to overdose. Just two milligrams of fentanyl is lethal; a sugar packet sized bag of pure fentanyl, for example, can kill 5,000 people. Accordingly, the CDC offers guidance on preventing occupational exposure to fentanyl and warns that exposure may be fatal.

Decontamination

In 2021, due to unintentional fentanyl overdoses in multiple states, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) assessed decontamination options. This 2021 EPA study and recent webinar reported on the efficacy of common decontaminants. The EPA rated Dahlgren Decon “best” among several other tested choices. State/local law enforcement can access federal grants for decontamination solutions like Dahlgren Decon through the Bureau of Justice Assistance’s (BJA) Comprehensive Opioid, Stimulant, and Substance Abuse Program (COSSAP).
